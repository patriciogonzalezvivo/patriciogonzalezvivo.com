Much of my work, and in many ways myself, emerges from an ongoing act of translation: a movement between disciplines, cultures, and ways of seeing. It begins with landmarks, with relations, with mapping.

In painting, one learns to locate fixed points: measured intervals of the face, the alignment of features, the geometry that underpins resemblance. These coordinates form a scaffold, an inherited cartography of observation.

Yet as the process unfolds, this structure gives way. The analytical dissolves into the perceptual: color becomes relational, planes reveal themselves through the direction of light, and the image begins to breathe beyond its measurements. One departs from the map and enters the sensorial terrain of experience. At its fullest, this becomes a form of communion: a silent exchange between painter and sitter, where presence supersedes description.

In this series, I shift the burden of mapping onto the machine. By delegating the task of measurement, **I allow myself to dwell more fully within the territory of perception.**

This practice places the work within a longer lineage of artists who have embraced emerging technologies not as replacements, but as liberating forces: from the optical devices of the Renaissance, to the innovations of Flemish oil painting, to the portable paint tube that opened the world to Impressionism. Each reflects a willingness to claim new tools and turn them toward the irreducible work of art-making.

My own exploration with drawing machines began with the construction of a [custom wall plotter for Skylines, my MFA thesis at Parsons School of Design in 2014](/2014/skylines/). Since then, I have approached the machine as a collaborator capable of extending my ability to see and create. For this body of work, I developed custom software that uses computer vision to analyze images, translating their latent structures into vector paths drawn by a plotter onto a primed canvas.

Once this cartography is laid down, I return to it with oils and brushes. Freed from the obligation of precision, I enter the painting as a space to inhabit rather than to describe. Gestural, intuitive, responsive.

Because digital information gravitates toward replicability, this method opens a field of iteration. Each variation becomes an opportunity to sharpen or distort the particular quality of a perception, to resist the copy, to insist on the singular.

The resulting paintings are hybrids: partly made by a machine, partly by my own hands. Yet the authorship remains unified. I am responsible for both the code and the gesture, both directed by a single vision. These works do not seek to replicate reality, but to evoke it, through a dialogue between systems, materials, and ways of seeing.
